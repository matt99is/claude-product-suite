# Bias and Quality Guardrails

Use this reference to reduce bias and keep UserTesting.com studies concise.

## Core principle

Give participants realistic goals, not step-by-step instructions. A test should reveal whether the experience supports the goal, not whether the participant can follow a script.

## Concise-test rules

- Default to 15-20 minutes for unmoderated tests.
- Use two to four focused tasks unless the user has a strong reason for more.
- Keep warm-up and wrap-up short.
- Ask only questions that serve the research goal.
- Recommend a one-participant pilot for each materially different audience segment when practical.
- Split large studies into separate tests rather than overloading one session.

## Audience and screener rules

- Start with the audience behavior that matters to the product decision.
- Use demographics only when they materially affect the decision or recruiting feasibility.
- Avoid screeners that make the desired answer obvious.
- Avoid over-screening so tightly that the test no longer represents the intended audience.
- Include exclusions for people whose expertise, employment, or prior exposure would distort the result.
- If comparing segments, keep tasks consistent enough that differences can be interpreted.

## Task wording rules

Prefer:

```text
You want to compare options and choose the plan that best fits your household. Show us what you would do.
```

Avoid:

```text
Click the Compare plans button, open the Recommended tab, and tell us how easy the improved plan selector is to use.
```

Check every task for:

- leading adjectives: improved, better, easy, obvious, clear, confusing, frustrating;
- path-revealing labels when findability is being tested;
- multiple unrelated goals in one task;
- yes/no wording without a useful follow-up;
- instructions that reveal what the team hopes participants will do;
- artificial scenarios that the target audience would not realistically face.

## Response mode rule

Prefer verbal responses for qualitative insight. Verbal responses preserve reasoning, hesitation, emotion, and context.

Use written responses sparingly for:

- short labels;
- rankings;
- structured answers;
- copy/paste values;
- answers the team needs to scan quickly.

Avoid long written responses, especially on mobile.

## Comparison rules

- Use neutral names such as Concept A and Concept B unless brand recognition is the point.
- Balance order when the platform or study setup allows it.
- Ask the same core task for each concept.
- Separate preference from comprehension, trust, and task success.

## Synthesis rules

- Treat small UserTesting.com studies as qualitative evidence unless the study was explicitly designed for quantitative analysis.
- Report patterns as patterns, not proof.
- Separate observed behavior from analyst inference.
- Include participant and task references for important findings.
- Keep findings short and prioritized.
- Use visuals only when they clarify the evidence.
- Do not let a chart imply statistical confidence that the sample cannot support.
