# Plain Writing Quality Bar

Use this reference for every writing-style task. It sets the target for clear,
useful prose.

## Quality checks

- **Purpose first.** Know what the writing must help the reader understand,
  decide, or do.
- **Front-load the point.** Put the main claim, ask, or action before supporting
  context.
- **Use concrete language.** Prefer specific nouns and verbs over vague
  abstractions such as "unlock value", "drive impact", or "elevate the
  experience".
- **Use active voice by default.** Name who does what unless the actor is
  unknown or irrelevant.
- **Keep structure scannable.** Use short paragraphs, direct headings, and lists
  only when they make comparison or action easier.
- **Vary rhythm naturally.** Mix sentence lengths by meaning, not by formula.
  Avoid every paragraph resolving into the same polished cadence.
- **Cut throat-clearing.** Remove setup phrases such as "In today's landscape",
  "It is important to note", and "At its core" unless they add needed context.
- **Keep the reader in view.** Choose words the audience would use or understand
  in that channel.
- **Protect evidence.** Do not make claims more certain, sourced, or universal
  than the input supports.

## Rewrite priorities

1. Correct meaning and factual integrity.
2. Reader task and decision support.
3. Specificity and clarity.
4. Natural voice and rhythm.
5. Polish.

Polish comes last. Smooth prose that hides weak meaning is still weak prose.

## Useful edit questions

- What is this sentence doing?
- Could a reader act on this?
- Which noun or verb is too vague?
- What would a real person say if they were responsible for this claim?
- Is the structure helping the reader, or just making the paragraph sound tidy?

## Source-informed principles

- GOV.UK content guidance: meet user needs, use clear language, and choose the
  right tone for the situation.
- Google developer style: be clear, concise, and friendly without being cute.
- Microsoft style: write conversationally, use active voice, and get to the
  point quickly.
- Nielsen Norman Group: users scan web content, so structure and front-loaded
  meaning matter.
- Orwell's plain-writing rules remain useful as a check against stale phrases,
  inflated diction, and words that do not earn their place.
